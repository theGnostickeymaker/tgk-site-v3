  <section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌸 Chapter 1: The Wheel of Birth and Death</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="pali">Anikkhanto 'haṁ saṁsāraṁ, sandhāvissaṁ anibbisaṁ;<br>
    Gahakāraṁ gavesanto, dukkhā jāti punappunaṁ.</span><br>
    <span>&ldquo;In countless births I have run,  
    seeking the builder of this house.  
    Painful is birth again and again.&rdquo;</span>
    <cite> The Dhammapada</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>In Buddhist cosmology, life is not a single thread but a vast wheel, <strong>saṃsāra</strong>, the endless cycle of birth, death, and rebirth. Each turn conditioned by karma, each return shaped by craving and ignorance.</p>

  <p>The soul, or rather, the stream of consciousness, clings to form, sensation, becoming. What is reborn is not a fixed &ldquo;self,&rdquo; but a flow of conditioned tendencies, endlessly chasing fulfillment, endlessly circling back.</p>

  <p>Why does the wheel spin? Because the mind grasps. Because desire burns. Because ignorance veils the path beyond.</p>

  <p>The Buddha&rsquo;s teaching is not to escape life, but to end the <strong>compulsion</strong> to repeat it. To step off the wheel by seeing clearly, acting purely, and loosening the fetters that bind us to return.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;The house-builder you have been seen!  
    You shall build no house again.  
    All your rafters are broken; the ridgepole is shattered.  
    The mind has attained the unconditioned.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> The Dhammapada</cite>
  </blockquote>
  </section>

  <section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR The Wheel of Birth and Death</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Saṃsāra</strong>; the endless cycle of birth, death, and rebirth shaped by karma.</li>
    <li>There is no fixed soul; only a conditioned stream of consciousness and tendencies.</li>
    <li>The wheel turns because of craving (taṇhā) and ignorance (avijjā).</li>
    <li>The Buddha&rsquo;s path teaches how to break the cycle and reach liberation (nibbāna).</li>
    <li>Escaping saṃsāra is not death, it is freedom from conditioned becoming.</li>
  </ul>
  </section>

<!--🌊 Chapter 2: Karma, The Seeds We Sow-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌊 Chapter 2: Karma: The Seeds We Sow</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="pali">Sabbe sattā kammassakā, kammadāyādā, kammayonī, kammabandhū, kammapaṭisaraṇā.</span><br>
    <span>&ldquo;All beings are owners of their deeds, heirs of their deeds, born of their deeds, related to their deeds, supported by their deeds.&rdquo;</span>
    <cite> The Anguttara Nikāya</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>In Buddhist thought, karma is not reward or punishment, it is <strong>cause and effect</strong>.</p>

  <p>Every thought, word, and action plants a seed. These seeds ripen over time, shaping the conditions of future experience. No god assigns fate; no external judge hands down decrees. We walk inside the garden we ourselves have sown.</p>

  <p>Crucially, karma is not fate. It is <strong>conditioned becoming</strong>. Old seeds shape the soil, but new choices can change the harvest.</p>

  <p>This is why the Buddha taught: <em>act with awareness</em>. Cultivate good roots: compassion, patience, wisdom. Weed out harmful roots: anger, greed, delusion.</p>

  <p>The chain of cause is unbroken, but at every link, there is choice.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;If you want to know your past, look at your present condition.  
    If you want to know your future, look at your present actions.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Buddhist proverb</cite>
  </blockquote>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR Karma: The Seeds We Sow</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Karma</strong> is cause and effect, the shaping of future conditions by present actions.</li>
    <li>It is not fate or punishment, but a natural law of causality.</li>
    <li>Old karma influences, but does not lock, the future, new actions matter.</li>
    <li>The Buddha&rsquo;s path encourages mindful planting: sowing wisdom, compassion, and awareness.</li>
    <li>Each moment offers a chance to break old cycles and choose new seeds.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<!--🌠 Chapter 3: Rebirth: The Wheel Turns-->
 <section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌠 Chapter 3: Rebirth: The Wheel Turns</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="pali">Na taṁ mātā pitā kayirā, aññe vā pi ca ñātakā;  
  Sammāpaṇihitaṁ cittaṁ, seyyaso naṁ tato kare.</span><br>
  <span>&ldquo;Neither mother, nor father, nor any other relative can do one greater good  
  than one&rsquo;s own well-directed mind.&rdquo;</span>
  <cite> The Dhammapada</cite>
  </blockquote>


  <p>In Buddhist cosmology, rebirth is not about a soul jumping body to body, it is the continuation of <strong>process</strong>.</p>

  <p>When conditions are right, consciousness arises again.  
  When old karma ripens, it shapes the next becoming.</p>

  <p>This is <em>saṃsāra</em>: the wheel of birth and death, spun by craving, ignorance, and clinging.</p>

  <p>The wheel turns through six realms, gods, titans, humans, animals, hungry ghosts, hell beings, but all are impermanent.  
  All are marked by dukkha: unsatisfactoriness.</p>

  <p>Rebirth is not destiny, but inertia. To break free, the Buddha taught, one must break the inner chains: craving, aversion, delusion.</p>

  <p>With insight and practice, the wheel can stop turning.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;Through many births I wandered on, seeking the builder of this house.  
    Painful is birth again and again.  
    But now, house-builder, you are seen!  
    You shall not build this house again.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> The Dhammapada</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR Rebirth: The Wheel Turns</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Rebirth</strong> is the continuation of conditioned consciousness, shaped by karma.</li>
    <li>It is not a permanent self, but a process of becoming.</li>
    <li>The wheel of saṃsāra turns through six realms, all marked by impermanence and suffering.</li>
    <li>Insight into craving and clinging is the key to liberation.</li>
    <li>When ignorance ends, the wheel stops turning, and freedom dawns.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>Anattā (no-self) distinguishes Buddhist rebirth: no eternal soul migrates, only a stream of consciousness shaped by karma, like a flame passing from candle to candle.</p>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌌 Chapter 4: Karma: The Law of Cause & Effect</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="pali">
      Sabbe sattā kammassakā, kammadāyādā, kammayonī, kammabandhū, kammapaṭisaraṇā.
    </span><br>
    <span>&ldquo;All beings are owners of their karma, heirs to their karma, born of their karma, related to their karma, abide supported by their karma.&rdquo;</span>
    <cite> The Buddha, Anguttara Nikāya</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>Karma is often misunderstood.</p>

  <p>It is not cosmic punishment or reward.  
  It is not a divine ledger keeping score.</p>

  <p>Karma means <strong>action,</strong> intentional action, shaped by mind. Every thought, word, and deed leaves an imprint, ike ripples on a vast sea.</p>

  <p>These ripples combine, amplify, collide, shaping not just the future, but the <em>shape</em> of the one who acts.</p>

  <p>The Buddha taught: &ldquo;What you are is what you have done. What you will be is what you do now.&rdquo;</p>

  <p>There is no fixed self, but there is momentum. Karma is the great sculptor, and mind is the chisel.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;With our thoughts, we make the world.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> The Dhammapada</cite>
  </blockquote>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR Karma: The Law of Cause & Effect</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Karma</strong> is intentional action, mental, verbal, physical.</li>
    <li>Every act leaves a trace, shaping future experiences and conditions.</li>
    <li>Karma is not fate or judgement, it is natural cause and effect.</li>
    <li>Good and bad karma condition future rebirths and mind states.</li>
    <li>Freedom begins by seeing karma clearly and acting with wisdom.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌊 Chapter 5: Samsāra: The Endless Wheel</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="pali">&ldquo;Long have you wandered through birth and death, like a traveler lost in the desert, forgetting the way home.&rdquo;</span><br>
    <cite> The Buddha, Saṁyutta Nikāya</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>Samsāra is the great wheel, the endless cycle of birth, death, and rebirth. It is not a place, but a <strong>process</strong>.</p>

  <p>Beings circle endlessly through worlds and forms, driven by karma, grasping, fearing, clinging, and forgetting.</p>

  <p>The human realm, the heavenly realms, the lower realms all are temporary stations, spun by ignorance and desire.</p>

  <p>Samsāra is not punishment. It is <em>inertia</em>. The rolling momentum of unawakened mind.</p>

  <p>Each rebirth offers new chances, but each is also bound by the same blind turning.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;Not until the root of craving is cut  
    does the wheel come to rest.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> The Dhammapada</cite>
  </blockquote>
</section>

    <li>It spans many realms, human, divine, animal, ghostly, hellish, none permanent.</li>
    <li>Ignorance, craving, and clinging keep the wheel spinning.</li>
    <li>The goal is not to &ldquo;win&rdquo; samsāra, but to transcend it through wisdom and release.</li>
    <li>Nirvāṇa is the end of samsāra, the stilling of the wheel.</li>

<!--🌊 Chapter 7: The Ocean of Samsāra-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌊 Chapter 7: The Ocean of Samsāra</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="pali">
      Dīghā jāti saṁsāraṁ, aññāṇaṁ arahato gatiṁ.
    </span><br>
    <span>&ldquo;Long is the cycle of birth and death for the fool who does not know the true path.&rdquo;</span>
    <cite> Dhammapada</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>Samsāra is the endless ocean of birth, death, and rebirth. It is the wheel that spins not because of fate, but because of <strong>clinging</strong>.</p>

  <p>We cling to identity, to pleasure, to permanence, to self. And so we return, lifetime after lifetime, chasing the mirage.</p>

  <p>The Buddha did not call this world evil, but he called it <em>unsatisfactory</em> (dukkha): a sea of constant change, where every wave rises and falls, never fully graspable.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;Not until the ocean is emptied, not until the mountains are ground to dust, will craving find satisfaction.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Buddhist Teaching</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>Samsāra cannot be escaped by running.  
  It is escaped by <strong>awakening</strong>.</p>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR The Ocean of Samsāra</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Samsāra</strong> is the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth, driven by craving and ignorance.</li>
    <li>It is not a punishment, but a pattern of clinging and becoming.</li>
    <li>The world is impermanent and unsatisfactory; chasing satisfaction only deepens the cycle.</li>
    <li>True escape comes through wisdom and non-attachment, not through external change.</li>
    <li>To cross samsāra is to wake up, not run away.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<!--🌸 Chapter 8: The Dharma Gate of Liberation-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌸 Chapter 8: The Dharma Gate of Liberation</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="sanskrit">धर्मद्वार</span><br>
    <span>&ldquo;The Dharma gate is boundless; I vow to enter it.&rdquo;</span>
    <cite> Zen Buddhist vow</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>The Buddhist afterlife is not just a passage, it is a <strong>teaching ground</strong>.</p>

  <p>Every bardo, every rebirth, every encounter with suffering is a <em>Dharma gate,</em> a threshold through which the soul can awaken.</p>

  <p>But these gates do not open automatically. They require <strong>presence</strong>, <strong>intention</strong>, and <strong>clear seeing</strong>.</p>

  <p>The awakened practitioner learns to pass through these gates while alive: in every moment of fear, attachment, or confusion,they ask, <em>What is this showing me? What is the Dharma here?</em></p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;When you meet the Buddha, kill the Buddha.  
    When you meet the patriarchs, kill the patriarchs.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Zen koan</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>In other words, do not cling, not even to awakening itself. The Dharma gate of liberation is boundless because it is ever-shifting.</p>

  <p>Every experience becomes a chance to <strong>unbind</strong> the self, to loosen the karmic chains, and to glimpse the radiant empty freedom beyond form.</p>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR The Dharma Gate</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Dharma Gate = Teaching Threshold</strong><br>Every challenge or experience is an entry point for awakening.</li>
    <li><strong>Boundless Paths</strong><br>There is no single route, the gates open endlessly, in every moment.</li>
    <li><strong>Koan Wisdom</strong><br>Don't cling, even to your own progress, liberation requires letting go of even the self that seeks.</li>
    <li><strong>Awakening as a Daily Practice</strong><br>Not just in the afterlife, but here and now, we walk through the Dharma gates.</li>
    <li><strong>Freedom Beyond Form</strong><br>Each gate reveals the spacious, unbound nature of ultimate reality.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<!--🪞 Chapter 9: The Mirror of Karma-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🪞 Chapter 9: The Mirror of Karma</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="sanskrit">कर्म</span><br>
    &ldquo;Karma is the mirror in which the soul sees its own face.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Buddhist proverb</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>In the Buddhist vision, the afterlife is not ruled by external judges. There is no divine figure weighing your fate.</p>

  <p>Instead, you stand before the <strong>mirror of karma</strong>: the reflection of your own deeds, intentions, and mental formations.</p>

  <p>Every action leaves an imprint. Every thought shapes the next unfolding. This is not punishment or reward, it is <em>resonance</em>.</p>

  <p>As the <strong>bardo soul</strong> navigates the liminal states, it encounters these karmic reflections. Pleasant forms arise from compassion and generosity. Frightening visions arise from anger, greed, or delusion.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;You are not punished for your anger, you are punished by your anger.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Buddhist teaching</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>But even here, the wise know: these visions are <strong>empty</strong>. They are projections of the mind.</p>

  <p>The awakened practitioner does not cling to the beautiful or fear the terrifying. They walk through the mirror, knowing: 
  <em>Karma arises, and karma passes away.</em></p>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR The Mirror of Karma</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Karma = Mirror</strong><br>You see the effects of your past actions reflected back to you, but no one else is judging you.</li>
    <li><strong>Resonance, Not Punishment</strong><br>The afterlife reveals the patterns you&rsquo;ve shaped, without moral condemnation.</li>
    <li><strong>Visions Are Mind</strong><br>Pleasant or frightening bardo experiences are mind-projections, not fixed realities.</li>
    <li><strong>Awakening Beyond Karma</strong><br>The liberated soul sees through the mirror, and does not get caught in reflection.</li>
    <li><strong>Action Still Matters</strong><br>While empty, karma shapes experience, so walk carefully, mindfully, compassionately.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<!--🌊 Chapter 10: The Gate of Rebirth-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌊 Chapter 10: The Gate of Rebirth</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="sanskrit">संसार</span><br>
    &ldquo;Samsāra: the endless flow of birth, death, and rebirth.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Buddhist teaching</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>At the end of the bardo journey, a gate waits: the <strong>Gate of Rebirth</strong>.</p>

  <p>If the soul has not achieved liberation, if it clings to desire, fear, or identity, it is drawn back into the <em>wheel of becoming</em>.</p>

  <p>This is not punishment. It is the natural momentum of karmic patterns.</p>

  <p>The Tibetan texts describe visions at this gate: couples embracing, the pull of attraction, the surge of craving. These are not merely images, they are the karmic hooks pulling the soul into a new womb, a new life, a new cycle.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;Not in the heavens, not in the middle of the sea, not by entering a cleft in the mountains, nowhere on earth is there a place 
    where one might escape the result of one&rsquo;s deeds.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Dhammapada 127</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>But for the awakened, the gate is not a trap. It is an opening.</p>

  <p>The practitioner with gnosis does not get caught.  
  They walk through the gate not into rebirth,  
  but beyond it into <strong>nirvāṇa</strong>.</p>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR The Gate of Rebirth</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Rebirth Is the Default</strong><br>Without awakening, the soul is drawn back by karmic momentum.</li>
    <li><strong>The Gate Is Craving</strong><br>Desire and attachment are the doorway into new becoming.</li>
    <li><strong>No External Judge</strong><br>Rebirth happens naturally, not imposed by a god.</li>
    <li><strong>Awakening Breaks the Cycle</strong><br>The liberated soul does not cling, and thus steps beyond the wheel.</li>
    <li><strong>Nirvāṇa Awaits</strong><br>Beyond rebirth is the unconditioned, the end of becoming, the final peace.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<!--🌌 Chapter 11: Nirvāṇa and the End of Becoming-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌌 Chapter 11: Nirvāṇa and the End of Becoming</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="sanskrit">निर्वाण</span><br>
    &ldquo;Nirvāṇa: the extinguishing, the unbinding, the peace beyond all fabrication.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Buddhist teaching</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>What lies beyond the gate, if the soul does not return?</p>

  <p>The Buddha called it <strong>nirvāṇa,</strong> a state not of annihilation, but of release.</p>

  <p>It is the end of becoming. The extinguishing of the fires: greed, hatred, delusion.</p>

  <p>In nirvāṇa, there is no more clinging, no more thirst, no more &ldquo;I&rdquo; trying to become.</p>

  <p>The <em>Theravāda</em> texts speak of it as the unconditioned: that which is not born, does not arise, and does not pass away.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;There is an unborn, an unbecome, an unmade, an unfabricated. If there were not this unborn, unbecome, unmade, unfabricated, 
    there would be no escape from the born, become, made, fabricated.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Udāna 8.3</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>Nirvāṇa is not a place. It is not a heaven, or a reward. It is the end of the need to grasp, to strive, to continue.</p>

  <p>It is peace. It is freedom.It is the ultimate cessation of suffering.</p>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR Nirvāṇa</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Nirvāṇa = Unbinding</strong><br>The extinguishing of the fires of desire, hatred, and delusion.</li>
    <li><strong>Not Annihilation</strong><br>Nirvāṇa is not non-existence; it is the end of suffering and becoming.</li>
    <li><strong>The Unconditioned</strong><br>It is the reality beyond birth and death, beyond all fabricated things.</li>
    <li><strong>Freedom Beyond Self</strong><br>The ego dissolves; what remains is peace without a centre.</li>
    <li><strong>The Goal of the Path</strong><br>All Buddhist practice aims toward this ultimate liberation.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<!--🌟 Chapter 12: The Bodhisattva&rsquo;s Vow-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌟 Chapter 12: The Bodhisattva&rsquo;s Vow</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="sanskrit">बोधिसत्त्व</span><br>
    &ldquo;Beings are numberless; I vow to save them. Delusions are inexhaustible; I vow to end them.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Bodhisattva vow</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>In the Mahāyāna tradition, the story shifts.</p>

  <p>Not all who stand at the gate choose to cross. Some turn back.</p>

  <p>These are the <strong>bodhisattvas,</strong> beings who have realised the emptiness of all things, who stand at the threshold of nirvāṇa, but choose to remain in the world of suffering.</p>

  <p>Why?</p>

  <p>For love.</p>

  <p>The bodhisattva sees that no being is separate. That liberation is incomplete if even one soul remains trapped in ignorance.</p>

  <p>So they take the vow: to delay their own final freedom until <em>all</em> beings are free.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;The one who realises emptiness sees no difference between self and other, between nirvāṇa and saṃsāra, and thus stays in the world as a lotus in the mud.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Mahāyāna teaching</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>This is the paradox of the highest path: the more the self dissolves, the more love remains.</p>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR Bodhisattva Vow</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Bodhisattva = Enlightened Being</strong><br>One who chooses to remain in the world out of compassion.</li>
    <li><strong>The Vow</strong><br>To save all beings, end all delusions, and walk the path endlessly for others.</li>
    <li><strong>Beyond Self-Liberation</strong><br>The bodhisattva sees no final nirvāṇa apart from the awakening of all.</li>
    <li><strong>Compassion Without Bound</strong><br>This is the heart of Mahāyāna Buddhism, love that refuses to leave anyone behind.</li>
    <li><strong>The Lotus in the Mud</strong><br>The bodhisattva lives in the world but is not stained by it, like a lotus blooming from the mire.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<!--🌿 Chapter 13: Emptiness and the Clear Light-->
<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">🌿 Chapter 13: Emptiness and the Clear Light</h2>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    <span class="sanskrit">शून्यता</span><br>
    &ldquo;Form is emptiness; emptiness is form.  
    Emptiness is not separate from form; form is not separate from emptiness.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Heart Sūtra</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>What lies at the core of all Buddhist realisation?</p>

  <p>Not a thing. Not a god.  
  But <strong>śūnyatā,</strong> emptiness.</p>

  <p>Emptiness is not void, not nothingness. It is the realisation that all things lack inherent, independent existence.</p>

  <p>Body, mind, thought, self, time, space all are woven from interdependence, arising and falling like waves on the ocean.</p>

  <blockquote class="blockquote">
    &ldquo;All dharmas are like a dream, an illusion, a bubble, a shadow, like dew or a flash of lightning, thus should you view them.&rdquo;<br>
    <cite> Diamond Sūtra</cite>
  </blockquote>

  <p>In Tibetan Buddhism, this emptiness reveals itself as the <strong>clear light,</strong> the luminous, formless awareness underlying all experience.</p>

  <p>It is not something to be added or achieved. It is what remains when clinging stops, when self dissolves, when the mind rests in its own nature.</p>

  <p>To realise emptiness is to awaken not into despair, but into boundless compassion, infinite openness, and the playful dance of the cosmos itself.</p>
</section>

<section class="section-block">
  <h2 class="section-heading">⚡ TL;DR Emptiness & Clear Light</h2>
  <ul class="list-emoji">
    <li><strong>Śūnyatā = Emptiness</strong><br>All things lack fixed, independent essence; they arise in interdependence.</li>
    <li><strong>Not Nihilism</strong><br>Emptiness is fullness, the openness that makes compassion and freedom possible.</li>
    <li><strong>The Clear Light</strong><br>The radiant, formless awareness underlying all phenomena, revealed when the mind is still.</li>
    <li><strong>The Heart of Mahāyāna</strong><br>Emptiness and compassion are inseparable; wisdom sees the empty nature, compassion embraces the world.</li>
    <li><strong>The Ultimate Dance</strong><br>Realising emptiness is awakening to the living, luminous play of existence.</li>
  </ul>
</section>
